The Hidden Cost of a Missed Call (and How to Calculate Yours)

Ask any service business owner what a missed call costs them. They'll tell you a number. It will be too low — usually by a factor of three or more. Here's the real math, and a formula you can run on your own business in ten minutes.

By Anthony Mora, Founder, Parkside Advisory Group

Every HVAC, plumbing, and electrical business owner I've talked to knows they miss calls. Most of them estimate the cost at something like "a few hundred bucks a job." Some say "a few thousand a month." A few shrug and say it's just the cost of doing business.

All of them are wrong in the same direction. The real number is bigger — often much bigger — because a missed call isn't one lost job. It's a compounding liability.

This essay walks through the real math: what a missed call is actually worth, why the true number is higher than most owners estimate, and a simple formula you can use to calculate the annual leak in your own business.

A missed call is not one lost job

Here's the first thing every owner underestimates. When you miss a call, you don't just lose the transaction. You lose the start of a relationship.

Service businesses earn most of their revenue from things that happen after the first job:

  • The furnace tune-up that turns into a blower motor replacement the following winter.
  • The service agreement the tech sells on the way out the door.
  • The referral to the neighbor three houses down.
  • The next four years of priority dispatch calls when anything in the house stops working.

All of that downstream revenue requires one thing: a successful first appointment. When the phone rings and nobody answers, the customer calls the next company on Google. You don't lose a $400 repair. You lose the $400 repair, the $280 tune-up next fall, the $3,800 system replacement three years from now, the service plan, and two of their neighbors.

That's why every credible customer-value study across the home services industry uses a lifetime value multiplier somewhere between 1.5x and 3x the first-job revenue. The lower end is for one-off specialty trades. The higher end is for HVAC, where service agreements and seasonal maintenance compound for years.

What one missed call is actually worth

Let's put numbers on it. These are conservative industry-typical ranges — your business will vary, but the order of magnitude holds.

HVAC residential. Average ticket on a service call is around $400. Close rate on a connected inbound call (someone who actually picks up and dispatches) is 60 to 80 percent. Lifetime value multiplier: 2 to 3x. That makes a single missed HVAC call worth roughly $480 to $960 in expected lost revenue on the first job alone, and $960 to $2,880 over the customer lifetime.

Plumbing residential. Average ticket is higher — call it $500 to $800 for a typical service call, with emergency calls running well over a thousand. Close rate on a connected call is 65 to 80 percent. LTV multiplier: 1.5 to 2.5x. Expected value of one missed call: $650 to $1,600 first-job, $1,000 to $4,000 lifetime.

Electrical residential. Average ticket varies widely but diagnostic-plus-repair lands around $350 to $600. Close rate: 60 to 75 percent. LTV multiplier: 1.5 to 2x. Expected value of one missed call: $260 to $500 first-job, $400 to $1,000 lifetime.

Two things jump out of these numbers. First: every missed call is worth somewhere between a few hundred dollars and several thousand. Second: the lifetime number is always at least double the transactional number. If your internal math only counts the first-job revenue, you're undercounting by half or more.

The Parkside formula

Here is the exact formula we walk every Revenue Leak Assessment through:

Annual Revenue Leak = Missed Calls/Week × 52 × Close Rate × Average Ticket × LTV Multiplier

Let's run it for a plausible small HVAC shop:

  • Missed calls per week: 15 (this is common — most owners who think they miss 3 a week are missing 10 to 20 once you check the phone logs)
  • Weeks per year: 52
  • Close rate on a connected call: 70%
  • Average ticket: $450
  • LTV multiplier: 2.5x

Math: 15 × 52 × 0.70 × 450 × 2.5 = $614,250 per year.

That's a small shop. A mid-size plumbing business with 30 missed calls a week, $650 average ticket, 75% close rate, and a 2x multiplier is leaking $1.52 million a year. These are not hypothetical numbers. They are the same ranges we produce when we run the assessment on a real business and reconcile against their actual phone logs.

Why the real number is higher than your estimate

Three reasons owners almost always guess low:

1. Missed calls are invisible

Unless you've specifically pulled a call log from your carrier or VoIP system, you don't know how many calls you're missing. The callers who get voicemail don't leave one half the time, and the ones who do leave one often don't hear back fast enough to wait. You can't estimate a number you can't see.

2. The office manager answers most of them

This is the most common cognitive trap. The owner hears the phone ring, the office manager answers, and everything feels fine. What the owner doesn't see is the 30% of calls that land while the office manager is on another line, at lunch, after 5pm, on Saturday morning, or out sick on Monday. The system has huge coverage gaps that only show up on the call log.

3. Lifetime value gets left out of the math

Even when owners count the missed first-job revenue, they rarely multiply it by the lifetime effect. A customer acquired today generates ten to twenty times more revenue over the next five years than the first ticket. Missed calls aren't transactional losses. They're customers your competitor just acquired.

The secondary costs nobody sees

Beyond the direct dollar leak, three secondary effects make missed calls worse than the formula suggests:

Reviews. Customers who can't reach you leave frustrated reviews on Google. Not the extreme kind — just the quiet two-star "couldn't get anyone to call me back" variety. These review patterns drag down your local map pack ranking, which reduces future inbound volume, which reduces revenue again. The leak compounds.

Paid ad waste. If you're running Google LSAs, Meta ads, or any form of paid acquisition, every missed call is money spent to generate a lead you then failed to capture. The cost-per-acquisition on your ads is 20 to 40 percent higher than the dashboard says, because the denominator should only include calls you actually answered.

Technician utilization. Fewer booked jobs means idle technician hours, which means higher labor cost per completed ticket, which means margin compression. The P&L shows it as "low utilization month." The real cause is upstream.

What a realistic fix looks like

The good news: this entire leak is fixable, and it doesn't require hiring additional office staff.

A properly installed AI missed-call system captures every inbound call the humans don't answer. It texts the caller back within seconds — usually before the caller has even finished walking away from the phone — acknowledges them by name, asks what they need, and either books them directly or routes an urgent call to the owner's phone.

In the HVAC example above, recovering even 40% of the missed calls at the same 70% close rate pulls $245,700 back into the business. Annually. From a system that costs less than a part-time CSR.

Not every missed call is recoverable — some callers do want a human voice, some are wrong numbers, and some are competitors shopping prices. But the recovery rate on a well-designed system is high enough that the math becomes obvious: the return on a few thousand dollars of setup and a few hundred a month of software is a six-figure revenue recapture on the first year.

The bottom line

Every service business is leaking revenue through missed calls. The leak is almost always larger than the owner thinks, because the real number includes lifetime customer value, review-driven ranking loss, and wasted ad spend — not just the transactional loss on the first job.

You can calculate your own leak in ten minutes. Pull your phone log, count the missed calls from the last week, and run the formula above. The result will either confirm that your current system is holding up, or — much more often — tell you exactly how much money is sitting on the table.

Either way, you'll know the number. Which is the first requirement for fixing anything.
